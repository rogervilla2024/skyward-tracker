Introduction

Gambling, including playing crash games like Skyward, carries inherent risks. This guide provides comprehensive information about responsible gambling practices, warning signs of problem gambling, and resources for those who need help.

The Most Important Message

Gambling should only ever be entertainment with money you can completely afford to lose. It should never be seen as a way to make money, solve financial problems, or escape from life's challenges.

Understanding the House Edge

The Mathematical Reality

Before discussing responsible gambling, it's crucial to understand why gambling always costs money over time:

  • Skyward's RTP is 96%: For every $100 wagered, $4 goes to the casino on average
  • This is not variable: No strategy, skill, or timing can change this
  • It's by design: Casinos are businesses designed to profit

Warning Signs of Problem Gambling

Financial Warning Signs

  • Gambling with money meant for bills, rent, or essentials
  • Borrowing money to gamble
  • Selling possessions to get gambling money
  • Using credit cards or loans for gambling
  • Having unexplained financial problems
  • Lying about money or finances

Behavioral Warning Signs

  • Spending more time gambling than intended
  • Chasing losses (trying to win back money lost)
  • Being preoccupied with gambling when not playing
  • Feeling restless or irritable when not gambling
  • Increasing bet sizes to feel the same excitement
  • Multiple failed attempts to stop or control gambling

Emotional Warning Signs

  • Gambling to escape problems or relieve negative emotions
  • Feeling guilt, anxiety, or depression about gambling
  • Lying to family or friends about gambling
  • Feeling defensive when confronted about gambling
  • Mood swings related to wins and losses

Social Warning Signs

  • Neglecting work, school, or family responsibilities
  • Withdrawing from friends and family
  • Missing important events due to gambling
  • Relationship problems caused by gambling
  • Reduced interest in previously enjoyed activities

Important

If you recognize any of these signs in yourself or someone you know, it may be time to seek help. Problem gambling is a recognized disorder, and effective help is available.

Self-Assessment Questions

Ask yourself these questions honestly:

  1. Have you ever gambled with money you couldn't afford to lose?
  2. Have you ever borrowed money or sold something to finance gambling?
  3. Have you ever lied to family or friends about your gambling?
  4. Have you ever felt the need to bet more and more money?
  5. Have you ever tried to win back money you've lost (chasing losses)?
  6. Have you ever felt restless or irritable when trying to reduce gambling?
  7. Have you ever gambled to escape problems or relieve anxiety/depression?
  8. Has gambling ever caused problems in your relationships?
  9. Has gambling ever affected your work or school performance?
  10. Have you ever felt guilty about gambling or what happens while gambling?

Assessment Guide

  • 0 "Yes" answers: You may be gambling responsibly, but stay aware
  • 1-2 "Yes" answers: You may be at risk - consider setting stricter limits
  • 3-4 "Yes" answers: You may have a gambling problem - consider seeking help
  • 5+ "Yes" answers: You likely have a gambling problem - please seek help

Responsible Gambling Practices

Before You Gamble

  • Set a budget: Decide how much you can afford to lose completely
  • Set time limits: Decide how long you'll play before you start
  • Never gamble under the influence: Alcohol and drugs impair judgment
  • Ensure it's entertainment: Only gamble if you're in a good mental state

While You Gamble

  • Stick to your budget: When it's gone, stop - no exceptions
  • Take breaks: Step away regularly to reassess
  • Don't chase losses: Losing is expected; accept it
  • Set win limits too: If you're ahead, consider stopping
  • Track your time: Use timers or alarms

After You Gamble

  • Track your results: Know exactly what you've won and lost over time
  • Be honest: Don't hide losses from yourself or others
  • Reflect: Did you enjoy it? Did you stay within limits?
  • Adjust: If you exceeded limits, consider why and make changes

Golden Rules

  1. Only gamble with money you can afford to lose
  2. Set limits BEFORE you start playing
  3. Never chase your losses
  4. Take regular breaks
  5. Don't gamble when emotional or intoxicated
  6. Balance gambling with other activities
  7. Accept that losing is the expected outcome

Responsible Gambling Tools

Casino-Provided Tools

Most reputable online casinos offer these features:

  • Cannot be increased immediately (cooling-off period)
  • Helps control spending

Loss Limits

  • Set maximum losses over a time period
  • Automatically stops play when reached
  • Prevents excessive losses

Session Time Limits

  • Set how long you can play
  • Get warnings or automatic logouts
  • Prevents extended sessions

Reality Checks

  • Periodic pop-ups showing time played and money spent
  • Forces you to acknowledge your activity
  • Interrupts "zone" state

Self-Exclusion

  • Voluntarily block yourself from the casino
  • Available for various periods (days, months, years, permanent)
  • Cannot be easily reversed

How to Use These Tools

  2. Set limits before you need them, not after problems arise
  3. Use the lowest limits you're comfortable with
  4. Enable all available responsible gambling features

Gambling Myths Debunked

Myth: "I can win money gambling if I play smart"

Reality: The house edge is mathematical and cannot be overcome with any strategy. The casino always has the advantage.

Myth: "I'm due for a win after losing"

Reality: This is the gambler's fallacy. Each game is independent; past results don't influence future outcomes.

Myth: "Gambling problems only happen to other people"

Reality: Problem gambling can affect anyone regardless of intelligence, income, or background.

Myth: "I can always stop when I want to"

Reality: Problem gambling is characterized by loss of control. Many people believe they can stop until they realize they can't.

Myth: "It's only a problem if I can't afford it"

Reality: Problem gambling isn't just about money. It affects mental health, relationships, and quality of life.

Myth: "I just need one big win to solve my problems"

Reality: This thinking leads to deeper problems. Even big wins are typically gambled away by those with gambling problems.

When to Stop Playing

Stop Immediately If:

  • You've reached your pre-set budget limit
  • You've reached your time limit
  • You're considering using money meant for bills or necessities
  • You're thinking about borrowing money to continue
  • You're feeling stressed, anxious, or upset
  • You're trying to win back losses
  • You're no longer enjoying yourself
  • You're playing under the influence of alcohol or drugs

Consider Stopping Altogether If:

  • Gambling is causing financial problems
  • Gambling is affecting your relationships
  • You've tried to cut back but couldn't
  • You're lying about your gambling
  • Gambling is your primary form of entertainment
  • You feel you need to gamble

Getting Help

You're Not Alone

Millions of people struggle with gambling problems. It's a recognized disorder with effective treatments. Seeking help is a sign of strength, not weakness.

Types of Help Available

Helplines

  • Free, confidential phone support
  • Available 24/7 in many cases
  • Can provide immediate support and referrals

Support Groups

  • Gamblers Anonymous and similar programs
  • Peer support from others who understand
  • In-person and online meetings available

Professional Counseling

  • Individual therapy with trained counselors
  • Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) is effective
  • Can address underlying issues

Treatment Programs

  • Inpatient and outpatient programs
  • Comprehensive treatment approaches
  • For severe gambling problems

Steps to Get Help

  1. Acknowledge the problem: Be honest with yourself
  2. Reach out: Call a helpline or talk to someone you trust
  3. Take immediate action: Self-exclude from gambling sites
  4. Seek professional help: Contact a counselor or treatment program
  5. Build support: Join a support group, involve trusted family/friends

Helping Someone Else

If You're Concerned About Someone

  • Express concern without judgment: Use "I" statements
  • Listen: Let them talk without interrupting or lecturing
  • Don't enable: Avoid lending money or covering their debts
  • Provide information: Share resources but don't force
  • Protect yourself: Set boundaries and don't neglect your own needs
  • Seek support: Resources exist for families affected by gambling

What Not to Do

  • Don't issue ultimatums unless you're prepared to follow through
  • Don't take over their finances without professional guidance
  • Don't expect instant change - recovery is a process
  • Don't blame yourself for their gambling
